## QUESTION 71: Weather - Warm Front “Looks Fine Here” Trap

**Topic:** Weather - Fronts and Trend-Based Planning  
**Airports:** KDAY (Dayton) → KCMH (Columbus)  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
You’re planning a late-morning VFR hop from Dayton (KDAY) to Columbus (KCMH) for a lunch meeting you “can’t miss.” The departure METAR is VFR with high clouds. The surface analysis shows a **warm front** moving northeast toward your route.

Your destination TAF shows lowering ceilings later in the period, and the area forecast mentions increasing moisture and light rain.

Your passenger says, “If it’s VFR now, we’re good—fronts are slow.”

**What weather trend should you expect ahead of a warm front, and how should that influence your go/no-go?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
Ahead of a warm front, I expect **deteriorating ceilings and visibility** with widespread layered clouds and precipitation developing *before* the front arrives. Conditions can go from VFR to MVFR/IFR gradually but steadily. For a VFR-only pilot, that trend matters more than “what it looks like right now.” If my ETA overlaps the deterioration window, I should delay, reroute, or cancel rather than trying to “beat it.”

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“Only the front itself is bad. If we launch before it arrives, we’ll stay VFR.”

**Why This Matters:**  
Warm fronts commonly produce widespread stratiform clouds, mist, and rain with **lowering ceilings ahead of the surface boundary**. That’s exactly the kind of “slow squeeze” that traps VFR pilots—each decision point seems reasonable until it isn’t. 14 CFR 91.103 requires considering forecasts and trends along the route, not just a snapshot METAR. The safe decision is based on the direction of change and whether you have large margins and real outs.

**Cram Mode:**  
Warm front = ceilings/vis often deteriorate *ahead* of the boundary. Don’t chase “VFR now” into a steady downward trend.

**Reference:** 14 CFR 91.103, AC 00-45H (Aviation Weather Services)  
**Link:** https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-91/subpart-B/section-91.103

## QUESTION 72: Weather - Cold Front Timing (Fast Changes)

**Topic:** Weather - Rapid Deterioration and Wind Shifts  
**Airports:** KICT (Wichita) → KTOP (Topeka)  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
You’re planning a VFR flight late afternoon. A **cold front** is forecast to cross your route near your ETA. Surface winds are increasing, cumulus is building, and you see a narrow line of showers on the horizon.

The destination TAF has a sharp wind shift and a tempo group for reduced visibility and lower ceilings around your arrival time.

**If you decide to “go anyway,” what specific cold-front hazards can show up quickly, even while it still looks VFR?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
Cold fronts can bring **rapid wind shifts, gusts, turbulence, and convective showers** with quick drops in visibility and ceilings. The biggest trap is assuming you’ll have time to react. Even a “thin line” can create strong mechanical turbulence and wind shear near the surface. I need a large buffer or I should delay/cancel.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“Cold fronts are obvious. I’ll see the bad weather and just turn around if needed.”

**Why This Matters:**  
Cold fronts can change conditions in minutes, not hours. The visual picture often lags the operational hazard—gust fronts and wind shifts can arrive before the rain. If you’re near pattern altitude when winds flip and gust, your workload spikes exactly when your margin is small. Good ADM means recognizing that “I can always turn back” is a weak plan if the front crosses behind you and closes your return route.

**Cram Mode:**  
Cold front = fast changes: wind shift + gusts + turbulence + quick MVFR/IFR. “Turn back” may not be available once it moves through.

**Reference:** AC 00-45H (Aviation Weather Services), Aviation Weather Handbook (Fronts)  
**Link:** https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/handbooks_manuals/aviation

## QUESTION 73: Weather - Stationary Front and “Endless Marginal”

**Topic:** Weather - Persistent Low Ceilings and Drizzle  
**Airports:** KORF (Norfolk) → KRIC (Richmond)  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
You’ve been waiting two days for weather to improve. A **stationary front** is draped across Virginia. Both airports have been bouncing between MVFR and IFR with light rain and mist.

Your friend says, “It’s not moving, so it can’t get worse. Let’s just go when it’s barely VFR.”

**Why can stationary fronts be especially dangerous for VFR planning?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
Stationary fronts can produce **long-lasting low ceilings, drizzle, mist, and poor visibility** with little improvement for many hours. The risk is launching on a brief VFR “break” and then getting trapped when it returns to MVFR/IFR—often with widespread low stratus that eliminates outs. The correct approach is to wait for a clear improvement trend, not chase short-lived fluctuations.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“If it’s been the same for days, it’s predictable and safe to squeeze a VFR flight in.”

**Why This Matters:**  
Stationary-front weather is often “barely legal” and persistent. That’s prime VFR-into-IMC territory because the **ceiling is the limiter**, not isolated cells you can go around. Even if you stay technically legal under 91.155, your safety margin can be razor thin, especially near terrain or at night. Stable, improving trends matter more than a momentary METAR.

**Cram Mode:**  
Stationary front = persistent low stratus/drizzle/mist. Don’t chase brief VFR breaks—wait for a real improvement trend.

**Reference:** 14 CFR 91.155, AC 00-45H (Aviation Weather Services)  
**Link:** https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-91/subpart-B/section-91.155

## QUESTION 74: Weather - Sea Breeze Boundary Surprise

**Topic:** Weather - Local Circulations and Pop-Up Convection  
**Airports:** KJAX (Jacksonville) → KSGJ (St. Augustine)  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
You’re doing a short coastal training flight in Florida. The morning is calm and sunny. By early afternoon, inland temps rise quickly. The winds at the coast start shifting onshore, and cumulus begins building in a line roughly parallel to the coastline.

Your CFI asks, “What’s causing that line, and why does it matter for your planning?”

**Explain what’s happening and what hazard can form quickly.**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
That’s a **sea breeze front/boundary**—cooler marine air pushing inland and lifting warm moist air. It can rapidly create **towering cumulus and thunderstorms** along the boundary, plus gusty shifting winds. It matters because conditions can change quickly from benign VFR to convective hazards along an otherwise short route.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“It’s a short flight near the coast. If storms build, they’ll be easy to avoid.”

**Why This Matters:**  
Local boundaries can generate convection faster than the big-picture forecast suggests. The first sign is often a line of building cumulus and a wind shift. If you wait until you “see lightning,” you’re late—gust fronts and turbulence can arrive earlier. Good ADM is anticipating that summer coastal environments can go from smooth to ugly within an hour and planning conservative turn-around points.

**Cram Mode:**  
Sea breeze boundary can rapidly trigger storms and gusty wind shifts. Coastal VFR can change fast—plan turn-around points early.

**Reference:** Aviation Weather Handbook (Local winds/boundaries), PHAK Chapter 12  
**Link:** https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/handbooks_manuals/aviation

## QUESTION 75: Weather - AIRMET Tango (Turbulence) and “It’s Just Bumps”

**Topic:** Weather - Turbulence Forecasts and Light Aircraft Risk  
**Airports:** KSLC (Salt Lake City) → KLGU (Logan)  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
You’re planning a VFR flight in Utah with mountain terrain nearby. There’s an **AIRMET Tango** for moderate turbulence below 12,000 feet due to strong winds aloft and mountain wave potential.

Your passenger says, “Moderate turbulence just means it’ll be uncomfortable. We can handle it.”

**What does “moderate turbulence” actually imply for a small trainer, and what’s your decision?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
In a light trainer, moderate turbulence can mean **hard-to-control altitude/airspeed variations** and a real risk of exceeding maneuvering speed limits if I’m not careful. It can also make passengers panic and increase workload. If the AIRMET aligns with terrain and strong winds aloft, I should delay, choose a smoother time of day, fly a lower-risk route, or cancel. If I go, I slow to Va when appropriate, secure the cabin, brief passengers, and have outs.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“AIRMETs are for airliners and IFR. In a 172, turbulence is just uncomfortable.”

**Why This Matters:**  
AIRMETs are specifically relevant to general aviation. Moderate turbulence can produce substantial changes in attitude/altitude and create structural/operational risk if you’re fast or distracted. In the mountains, turbulence can be unpredictable and localized—especially on the lee side. This is as much an ADM problem as a weather knowledge problem: recognizing when the risk-to-benefit ratio is poor for a training flight.

**Cram Mode:**  
AIRMET Tango is GA-relevant. Moderate turbulence in a 172 isn’t “just bumps”—it’s workload + control + Va discipline + real risk.

**Reference:** AIM Chapter 7 (AIRMETs), Aviation Weather Handbook (Turbulence)

## QUESTION 76: Weather - Non-Convective SIGMET (Severe Icing/Turbulence)

**Topic:** Weather - SIGMET Severity and VFR Implications  
**Airports:** KMSP (Minneapolis) area  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
You’re planning a VFR flight on a winter day. Weather is currently VFR at your departure airport. Your briefing shows a **SIGMET** for severe icing and/or severe turbulence in a large area that includes part of your planned route.

Your friend says, “SIGMETs are mostly an IFR thing. If we stay VFR, we won’t get icing.”

**How should a SIGMET change your plan—even for VFR?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
A SIGMET indicates **hazardous weather significant to all aircraft**. Severe icing or turbulence can exist even when surface conditions look VFR, and the consequences for a light trainer are unacceptable. Even if I *intend* to stay out of clouds, routes often force climbs/descents near moisture layers. The practical decision is no-go or a major reroute away from the SIGMET area.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“VFR avoids weather. If I’m legal under 91.155, SIGMETs don’t apply to me.”

**Why This Matters:**  
SIGMETs exist to highlight conditions that can be dangerous regardless of flight rules. Severe icing/turbulence can rapidly overwhelm a small aircraft. “I’ll stay VFR” is not a guarantee—visibility can drop, cloud bases can lower, and you can be forced into moisture during normal operations. Good decision-making treats SIGMETs as a red flag requiring avoidance, not a footnote.

**Cram Mode:**  
SIGMET = significant hazard to ALL aircraft. VFR isn’t an immunity badge—avoid the area or don’t go.

**Reference:** AIM 7-1-6 (SIGMETs), 14 CFR 91.103

## QUESTION 77: Weather - Freezing Rain (Warm Front “Hidden Killer”)

**Topic:** Weather - Icing Type Recognition and No-Go  
**Airports:** KSDF (Louisville) → KLEX (Lexington)  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172 (not approved for known icing)

**Scenario:**  
You’re planning a winter VFR flight. The surface temps at both airports are 34–36°F, and the sky is overcast with light precipitation. The forecast discussion mentions a warm layer aloft over subfreezing air at higher elevations and a chance of **freezing rain** north of your route.

Your friend says, “If it’s above freezing on the ground, icing won’t be an issue.”

**Why is freezing rain such a big red flag, and what’s your decision?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
Freezing rain is a major no-go because it can produce **rapid, severe clear icing**. Surface temps being slightly above freezing doesn’t protect me at altitude where temperatures can be below 0°C. Freezing rain implies a warm layer aloft with supercooled droplets that freeze on contact—ice can accumulate extremely fast. In a non-FIKI 172, I avoid this setup entirely.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“Freezing rain only matters if the surface is below freezing. We’re above freezing, so it’s safe.”

**Why This Matters:**  
Freezing rain indicates a vertical temperature structure that creates large supercooled droplets—worst-case icing. You don’t need to be in “deep clouds” for this to be dangerous; even light precip can coat the airplane quickly. This is one of those weather products where the correct answer is simple: don’t try to outsmart it.

**Cram Mode:**  
Freezing rain = clear-ice factory. Surface above freezing doesn’t matter—aloft can be below 0°C. Non-FIKI: avoid completely.

**Reference:** Aviation Weather Handbook (Icing/Freezing precipitation), 14 CFR 91.103  
**Link:** https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-91/subpart-B/section-91.103

## QUESTION 78: Weather - Carburetor Icing on a “Nice Day”

**Topic:** Weather - Carb Ice Risk Without Visible Moisture  
**Airports:** KCHS (Charleston) local flight  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172 (carbureted engine)

**Scenario:**  
It’s a mild spring day: temperature 65°F, dewpoint 62°F, light winds, and scattered clouds. You depart for a local practice flight. During descent at reduced power, the engine starts running rough and RPM drops.

Your passenger says, “How can icing happen? It’s warm and not raining.”

**What’s the likely cause and the immediate action?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
Likely **carburetor icing** due to high humidity and the temperature drop in the carb throat, especially at reduced power. Immediate action: apply **carb heat** (expect a further RPM drop briefly), keep it on until the engine runs smooth, and adjust power/mixture as needed. Then reassess whether to continue or return.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“Icing only happens near freezing temperatures or in clouds. This must be bad fuel or a mechanical failure.”

**Why This Matters:**  
Carb ice can form at surprisingly warm temperatures when humidity is high—especially during low-power operations. Misdiagnosing it wastes time while the engine continues losing power. Correct, timely use of carb heat is a core weather-and-systems crossover skill for checkrides: recognize the conditions and fix the problem immediately.

**Cram Mode:**  
Warm + humid + low power = carb ice risk. Rough engine/RPM drop in descent? Carb heat first.

**Reference:** PHAK Chapter 7 (Engines/Carb ice), POH  
**Link:** https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/handbooks_manuals/aviation/phak

## QUESTION 79: Weather - Altimeter Setting and “It’s Only 0.10”

**Topic:** Weather - Pressure Effects and Terrain Clearance  
**Airports:** KBOZ (Bozeman) arrival  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
You’re flying VFR near mountainous terrain. The altimeter setting at your departure was 30.20, but at your destination it’s 29.60 with a strong pressure gradient behind a front. You forget to update your altimeter setting for 30 minutes while descending into the valley.

Your friend says, “Altimeter settings are small details. A tenth of an inch doesn’t matter.”

**What’s the safety implication of not updating the altimeter setting in this environment?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
Not updating the altimeter setting can put you **hundreds of feet lower than you think**, reducing terrain clearance. In the mountains, that’s a big deal. “High to low, look out below”—if pressure decreases and you don’t reset, your true altitude is lower than indicated. I should update settings frequently when there’s a strong gradient and maintain extra terrain margin.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“My GPS shows altitude anyway, so the altimeter setting is mostly irrelevant.”

**Why This Matters:**  
In mountain/valley environments, small errors compound with stress and reduced margins. GPS altitude isn’t a substitute for proper instrument setup in normal operations, and not every GPS altitude is baro-corrected. The core checkride lesson is disciplined procedures: update altimeter settings when available, cross-check, and protect terrain clearance.

**Cram Mode:**  
High to low, look out below. Big pressure drops + no reset = you’re lower than you think—dangerous near terrain.

**Reference:** PHAK Chapter 8 (Altimeter), AIM altimeter procedures

The trap is assuming scattered clouds are easy to stay clear of. Over terrain, cloud bases can vary and lower over ridges, forcing you to choose between **scud running** and climbing into reduced cloud clearance margins. Many VFR-into-IMC accidents begin with “I’ll just stay under it” while the route becomes a narrowing tunnel. Good ADM uses personal minimums that incorporate terrain and “what if it lowers 500 feet.”

## QUESTION 81: Weather - Virga and Sudden Wind Shifts

**Topic:** Weather - Microburst Risk in Dry Air  
**Airports:** KABQ (Albuquerque) area  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
You’re flying in the Southwest on a hot afternoon. You see rain shafts falling from clouds but evaporating before reaching the ground (**virga**). The surface winds at the airport suddenly increase and shift direction, and you see blowing dust.

Your passenger says, “It’s not even raining on the ground—why is the wind getting so crazy?”

**What hazard can virga indicate, and what’s the correct action near takeoff/landing?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
Virga can indicate strong downdrafts and **microburst/outflow** potential. Evaporative cooling accelerates descending air, which then spreads out as a gust front at the surface, causing sudden wind shifts and shear. Near takeoff/landing, that’s a big hazard. I should delay departure/landing, expect wind shear, and avoid the area until the virga/outflow passes.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“No rain on the runway means no storm threat.”

**Why This Matters:**  
The danger isn’t the rain—it’s the vertical wind and outflow. Virga is a visual cue that the atmosphere is primed for strong downdrafts. Light aircraft are especially vulnerable near the ground when the wind changes faster than you can correct. Good ADM is recognizing the cue and refusing to “time it.”

**Cram Mode:**  
Virga = evaporative cooling = downdraft/outflow risk. No rain on the ground doesn’t mean no microburst hazard.

**Reference:** Aviation Weather Handbook (Thunderstorms/Microbursts)  
**Link:** https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/handbooks_manuals/aviation

## QUESTION 82: Weather - Squall Line vs “A Few Cells”

**Topic:** Weather - Convective Organization and Route Planning  
**Airports:** KMEM (Memphis) → KBNA (Nashville)  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
Radar shows a long, solid line of thunderstorms oriented north-south and moving east. The line crosses your entire route corridor. You can see towering clouds and a shelf-like feature on the leading edge.

Your friend says, “We’ll just go around the worst part or find a gap.”

**What makes a squall line fundamentally different from isolated cells for VFR planning?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
A squall line often behaves like a **moving wall**: widespread severe turbulence, strong winds, wind shear, and embedded cores with limited safe gaps. Even if a “gap” exists now, it can close quickly. The correct decision is to delay, reroute widely, or cancel—treat it as a barrier, not a slalom course.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“Thunderstorms are point hazards. If I’m patient, I can pick my way through.”

**Why This Matters:**  
Organized convective lines can outpace your ability to “manage” them in a light aircraft. The hazards extend beyond the rain core (gust fronts, anvil, shear), and the line often forces you into longer deviations with fuel/time pressure—exactly when pilots make poor decisions. The checkride-grade answer is recognizing convective organization and choosing not to engage.

**Cram Mode:**  
Squall line = barrier, not slalom. Gaps can close; hazards extend wide. Delay/reroute big or don’t go.

**Reference:** AIM Chapter 7 (Thunderstorms), Aviation Weather Handbook  
**Link:** https://www.faa.gov/air_traffic/publications/atpubs/aim_html/chap7_section_1.html

## QUESTION 83: Weather - Radar Attenuation (The “Clear Behind It” Illusion)

**Topic:** Weather - Interpreting Heavy Returns Correctly  
**Airports:** KOKC (Oklahoma City) area  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
On your EFB radar, you see a strong red core of precipitation. Behind it (downrange), the display looks relatively clear. Your passenger says, “Once we get past the red part, it’s fine.”

**Explain why “clear behind heavy red” can be misleading, and what you do instead.**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
Heavy precipitation can cause **radar attenuation**, where the signal is weakened and the display under-represents what’s behind the core. The “clear” area might actually be more storms. The safe response is conservative separation from the entire convective region and not using a single radar picture to justify penetration.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“If radar shows clear, it’s clear. Red is the only dangerous part.”

**Why This Matters:**  
Radar products—especially mosaics—have limitations. Attenuation and processing can hide the true structure of storms. The checkride-quality thinking is: if you’re close enough to care about attenuation, you’re already too close. Keep wide separation and avoid relying on “clear behind it” reasoning.

**Cram Mode:**  
Heavy core can hide what’s behind it (attenuation). Don’t trust “clear downrange” near strong returns—stay well clear.

**Reference:** AIM Chapter 7 (Weather radar/datalink limitations), Aviation Weather Handbook  
**Link:** https://www.faa.gov/air_traffic/publications/atpubs/aim_html/chap7_section_1.html

In mountainous areas, that’s a terrain trap. Good ADM recognizes that “legal VFR” can still be a bad decision if your visual cues are degraded.

**Cram Mode:**  
Smoke/haze can be “legal but unsafe” VFR—poor contrast/horizon/terrain cues. Use higher personal vis mins and divert early.

## QUESTION 85: Weather - Dewpoint Spread and “Fog Later”

**What does a rapidly shrinking T/Td spread suggest, and what’s the risk for your return plan?**

**Cram Mode:**

## QUESTION 86: Weather - Advection Fog on the Coast (Doesn’t “Burn Off”)

**Topic:** Weather - Fog Types and Persistence  
**Airports:** KSFO (San Francisco) area  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
It’s midday and cool marine air is flowing inland. The coast is socked in, and the METAR shows low ceilings with mist. Your friend says, “Just wait until noon—fog always burns off.”

**Why can coastal fog persist all day, and how do you plan around it?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
Coastal fog is often **advection fog** driven by a steady supply of cool moist air moving over a cooler surface and being pushed inland. If the onshore flow continues, it may *not* burn off—especially near the coast. Planning requires choosing inland alternates, watching wind direction/strength, and not assuming a time-based improvement.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“All fog is radiation fog. Sun = guaranteed improvement.”

**Why This Matters:**  
Different fog types behave differently. Advection fog can be stubborn because the mechanism isn’t overnight cooling—it’s ongoing moisture transport and cooling. Treating all fog like “morning fog” leads to bad planning and time pressure.

**Cram Mode:**  
Coastal advection fog can persist all day if onshore flow continues. Don’t use “it’ll burn off by noon” as a plan.

**Reference:** Aviation Weather Handbook (Fog types), PHAK Chapter 12  
**Link:** https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/handbooks_manuals/aviation

## QUESTION 87: Weather - Upslope Flow and Low Ceilings

**Topic:** Weather - Terrain-Induced Cloud Formation  
**Airports:** KCOS (Colorado Springs) local area  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172

**Scenario:**  
Winds are blowing steadily from the plains toward the foothills. The sky looks “mostly okay” over the airport, but you notice low clouds forming and thickening against the rising terrain west of town.

Your CFI asks, “Why are ceilings lower near the foothills even when it’s better over the plains?”

**Explain the mechanism and the operational hazard.**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
That’s **upslope flow**: air forced upward by terrain cools, moisture condenses, and cloud bases lower near the slope. Operationally, it can create localized MVFR/IFR near terrain and passes while nearby areas remain VFR. It’s a trap for VFR pilots who follow valleys toward rising terrain and watch ceilings drop as they go.

**Trap Reasoning:**

**Cram Mode:**  
Upslope wind = forced lift = cooling/condensation = lower ceilings near terrain. VFR can deteriorate fast as you approach foothills/passes.

**Cram Mode:**

**Cram Mode:**  
Strong winds aloft + terrain = turbulence/shear near the surface. Surface wind alone can hide the real risk—plan for lee-side rotor and go-around early.

## QUESTION 98: Weather - Icing Type Recognition (Rime vs Clear vs Mixed)

**Topic:** Weather - Recognizing and Responding to Ice  
**Airports:** KSYR (Syracuse) area  
**Aircraft:** Cessna 172 (not approved for known icing)

**Scenario:**  
You unintentionally pick up ice in visible moisture at subfreezing temperatures. The ice looks rough and opaque on the leading edge at first. Later it becomes smoother and more glassy.

Your passenger asks, “Does the type of ice matter?”

**What’s the difference between rime and clear ice, and why is clear ice particularly dangerous?**

**MODEL ANSWER:**  
Rime ice forms from small droplets and tends to be rough/opaque, building forward into the airflow. Clear ice forms from larger supercooled droplets that spread and freeze as a smooth, heavy coating. Clear ice is dangerous because it’s heavier, can form beyond protected surfaces, and can change airfoil shape rapidly, severely degrading lift and increasing stall speed. In a non-FIKI aircraft, any ice is a cue to exit icing conditions immediately.

**Trap Reasoning:**  
“Ice is ice. If it looks smooth, it’s probably not that bad.”

**Why This Matters:**  
Different icing types imply different droplet sizes and accumulation behavior. Clear ice can overwhelm the aircraft quickly and is harder to see early. Checkrides want you to connect the observation to action: don’t debate the taxonomy—get out of it.

**Cram Mode:**  
Rime = rough/opaque; clear = smooth/heavy and often worse. In non-FIKI: any ice = exit now.
